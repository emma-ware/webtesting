To test, we are basically building three models:

  • H0 - (Null hypothesis) there is no predictive relationship between HPI and any of these variables
  • H1 - there is a predictive relationship
  • H2 - there is a predicitive relationship even without new_units_per_capita_over_Five_yrs, median_net_wealth, and median_home_value

Based on the MANOVA tests, each of these variables meets the test for significance. Let’s look at some other tests, starting with linearHypothesis

lh.out <- linearHypothesis(h2, hypothesis.matrix = c("x.median_income = 0", "x.new_units_per_capita_over_Five_yrs = 0", "x.percent_usable_land = 0", "x.Ten_yr_HPI_percent_change = 0"))
lh.out

The further tests strongly support the H2 hypothesis that the five limited variables have significant independent effect on the model. With confidence that we have a good set of variables, we can start using the H2 model to make predictions.

Notes

In terms of next steps, we have 5 total models. 3 focus on predicting HPI, while 2 focus on HPI change. From the standpoint of developing the ability to assess future growth, HPI change is the more important response variable of the two. The predictor variables that our best subsets regression revealed demonstrate that variables focused on quantities and ratios related to units and land, along with measures of household consumption capacity and past HPI change will be key to effectively modeling housing demand.

With population change variables added, our model optimization suggests that one possible rule of thumb for assessing demand is that renters have a general bias towards home ownership, and that especially higher income renters are more likely to leave after sustained periods of supply~demand imbalance

Future Variables

One future test I would like to do is identify a variable that corresponds to impact of previous period’s HPI change (and the underlying signal) on expected demand in the current period. From [https://www.nber.org/papers/w21667], rapid increases in overall HPI are related to increases in willingness to spend on housing - moreso for those with high income and high leverage than other groups. It’s very possible that other variables in combination (for example usable land + renter proportion + historical population change) already explain the same variance that this elasticity variable explains.

Next Steps

Create a growth function for each variable in order to predict next year’s value. To keep things simple, we can base that growth function on a regression with the variable in question as the Y and 1yr HPI Change as the X (test to see if stronger adjusted R^2 and lower cross validation error rates with HPI as the X). This way, we can build the simulation in 2 steps

  • Step 1 - predict HPI change (or HPI)
  • Step 2 - predict change in each of the other variables

This approach (incorrectly) assumes that growth for each variable is independent of other variables - in reality, all of the variables are interrelated. However, our goal is not an exact replica of the real-world, but rather to identify consistent rules of thumb for making better projections with less (but laser focused) predictor variables/data than our market competitors likely have at their disposal.
